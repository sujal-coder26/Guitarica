Tranpose

When you transpose a song, it sounds lower or higher than it did originally. If you transpose a song composed in the key of C major to the key of D major, for example, the pitch will be higher. The key of D is a whole tone higher than the key of C.
Musicians, composers, performers, and arrangers transpose a song for a variety of reasons. You may make a tune that is suited for your vocals by transposing a musical composition. Some vocalists, for example, may feel the notes of a song to be too high or too low. The vocalist will find it simpler to execute the music piece if the song is transposed. It will also sound better since the instrument's tone and the vocalist's pitch are in sync.

Transposing a Song: A Step-by-Step Guide.

  1. Determine the key of the song. i.e. key of C.
  2. Determine the chord progression. i.e. C-F-G
  3. Determine the new key. i.e key of G.
  4. Determine the interval between the keys. i.e. C to G is 5 steps (including sharps)
  5. Shift the chords and notes by the interval.
  6. By shifting, the new progression will be formed. i.e. G-C-D (perfect 5 steps)

Using a Capo

A capo is essentially a clamp that can be used anywhere on the fretboard. You open the 'clamp' and place it on the fretboard where you want it. The capo then pushes all the strings down when you close it.
Capo changes the guitar's key. If you put a capo on the second fret and play a C chord, the sound that comes out of your guitar will be D. You'd be holding a C chord form, but the actual chord that would be heard would be D since the capo has raised the guitar's key. See the image at right for more clarification.

A capo is your best buddy if you're a serious singer-songwriter. Assume you've composed a song with a memorable melody and powerful chords. However, you're having trouble singing it correctly. You may swiftly change the key of a song by changing the capo to a different fret while using a capo.
